research & methodology

Could life travel between solar systems?

A digital twin of interstellar lithopanspermia — integrating simplified gravitational transport modeling with a biologically informed survival function.

Introduction

The idea of life spreading through the universe can be traced back to antiquity, later developing into the concept of lithopanspermia following the discovery of exoplanets (Mitton, 2022), which hypothesizes that life could be transferred between planetary systems via meteorites.

Transfer and capture of potentially life-harboring meteorites have been investigated within our Solar System (Worth et al., 2013), while further analyses indicate that exchange of solid material may be viable even on galactic scales (Ginsburg et al., 2018), strengthening the physical plausibility of lithopanspermia. Moreover, microorganisms such as Bacillus subtilis and Deinococcus radiodurans have demonstrated survival under space conditions on the EXPOSE facility aboard the International Space Station (Horneck et al., 2012; Vaishampayan et al., 2012), including survival while embedded in artificial meteorites (Panitz et al., 2014) and under extreme landing pressures (Zhao et al., 2026).

While these findings support individual components of the lithopanspermia hypothesis, namely material transfer and microbial survival, there are currently very few integrative models that combine probabilistic interstellar transport dynamics with trajectory-dependent biological survivability.

Our project addresses this gap by developing a digital twin of interstellar lithopanspermia, integrating simplified gravitational transport modeling with a biologically informed survival function. We want to answer the question: what is the probability of inter-system meteorite transfer resulting in viable microbial arrival? Furthermore, could life originate in a planetary system as a consequence of lithopanspermia, and under what probabilistic conditions?

By framing survival chances as a probabilistic process rather than a deterministic event, this model enables rapid hypothesis testing and parameter exploration, contributing a versatile conceptual and computational framework to origins-of-life research.

Assumptions

Physical

  • Ejecta are launched from Mars.
  • Interstellar travel time is the primary driver of accumulated radiation exposure.
  • The meteorite is a sphere with life embedded inside.

Biological

To model viability, we base calculations on bacterial endospores, one of the most durable known biological forms. Endospores originate from prokaryotic cells, which represent evolutionarily early and resilient life forms, making them suitable proxies for primitive transferable life. Their durability is attributed primarily to: multilayered protective coats, low core water content and DNA-protection mechanisms.

Rather than modeling absolute cell numbers, we model the fraction of the surviving population (N/N₀). This abstraction increases versatility for different data inputs and uses.

Factors included in survival modeling function:

  • Radiation exposure (accumulating over time)
  • Galactic cosmic rays
  • Natural radioactivity within meteorites
  • DNA hydrolysis, temperature-dependent

Factors not explicitly modeled (& rationale):

  • Solar UV radiation, diffuse X-rays, solar particles — minimal shielding is sufficient: a couple of mm blocks UV completely, <0.5 g/cm² blocks diffuse X-rays, ~30 g/cm² blocks solar particles. Meteorite dimensions and microbe placement are therefore modeled assuming sufficient internal shielding.
  • Vacuum — vacuum causes desiccation and rapid death (Nussinov & Lysenko, 1983; He et al., 2018). However, meteoroid ejection melts outer layers, which then rapidly solidify in space. This may create sealed internal microenvironments. We assume cargo is located within such protected regions.
  • Temperature and pressure during launch and landing — low-shock ejecta capable of preserving biological material have been proposed (Melosh, 1984). We assume all cargo on ejecta is initially viable. For landing, we assume selection already acted upon microbiota during ejection, increasing resilience towards temperature and pressure (von Hegner, 2021). Our primary focus is on inter-system transport probability and survival during travel.

Methodology

Physical

Data for meteorites was taken from JPL Small Body Database API. We outlined 12 types of meteorites: basalt, 3 types of chondrite, olivine, enstatite, iron-nickel, hydrated silicate, organic rich, ice rich, rubble pile, stony-iron.

The physical subsystem models simplified ejecta trajectories in a restricted N-body environment representing our solar system, interstellar space, and the target stellar system. Parameters explored include:

  • Ejection velocity
  • Travel time
  • Distance between systems
  • Capture probability

The output of this module is a probability distribution of travel times and arrival likelihood, which directly informs biological survival modeling.

Biological Component — Surviving Fraction Function

We model survival fraction using a time-dependent exponential decay function consistent with a Weibull-type survival approach:

N/N₀ = e−(b · Drad + C · Khyd) · t

Where:

  • b = radiation sensitivity coefficient
  • Drad = cumulative radiation dose (space + internal decay)
  • C = hydrolysis sensitivity coefficient
  • Khyd = temperature-dependent hydrolysis rate
  • t = time since ejection

Effect of radiation on survival

To estimate microbial radiation vulnerability, we used published datasets on Bacillus subtilis and Deinococcus radiodurans (Mileikowsky et al., 2000). Linear regression (y = a + bx) was applied to determine coefficient b, assuming a = 0 (no radiation → no radiation-induced mortality). The resulting range: b ∈ [0.15, 0.5]. It further allows for parameter exploration and sensitivity analysis rather than a single deterministic value.

Effect of hydrolysis on survival

Assuming release of 0.1% of DNA bases allows survival of 30% of bacteria:

S(t) = e−(C · K · t)
C = −ln(S) / (K · t)
For our case: C = −ln(0.3) / (0.001 · 1200)

Limitations

1. Simplified Gravitational Dynamics

The transport model uses a restricted N-body approximation and simplified ballistic trajectories. It does not incorporate full galactic dynamics, stellar cluster evolution, detailed gravitational perturbations from passing stars, or interactions with interstellar medium drag. Travel times and capture probabilities should be interpreted as order-of-magnitude estimates.

2. Abstract Representation of Stellar Systems

The digital twin represents two generic stellar systems rather than specific observed systems. While this increases generalizability, results reflect parameter-space exploration rather than case-specific modeling.

3. Biological Proxy Assumptions

The model assumes endospores as representative transferable life forms. This excludes non-spore-forming extremophiles, assumes Earth-like biochemistry, and does not account for unknown extraterrestrial life chemistries. Survival coefficients are derived from laboratory-based datasets under controlled conditions.

4. Survival Function Simplifications

The survival model assumes continuous exponential decay, linear radiation-dose relationships, and uniform shielding. In reality, radiation damage may be nonlinear, shielding heterogeneous, and DNA repair mechanisms may alter survival curves. Synergistic effects between radiation and hydrolysis are not modeled.

5. Exclusion of Certain Environmental Factors

The model does not simulate vacuum-induced molecular degradation over extreme timescales, micrometeoroid erosion, chemical alteration in the interstellar medium, or detailed thermal cycling. These factors may further reduce survival probabilities.

6. No Modeling of Post-Arrival Colonization

The model evaluates viable arrival, not successful colonization or abiogenesis initiation. It estimates transfer probability, not probability of life emergence.

7. Uncertainty in Long-Timescale Extrapolation

Empirical microbial survival data are extrapolated far beyond laboratory-tested timescales. Interstellar travel may span millions of years, introducing uncertainty when extending experimentally derived decay parameters.

Conceptual Limitation

Most importantly, the model remains probabilistic and exploratory. It does not claim to demonstrate that lithopanspermia occurs, but rather quantifies the parameter space under which it may remain non-zero. This limitation is also a strength: the digital twin is designed as a hypothesis-testing framework, not a deterministic predictive tool.
